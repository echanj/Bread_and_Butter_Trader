Overview

Our algorithmic trading journey has culminated in the development of a robust, fully automated backtesting and screening engine. This system is built around a variety of advanced concepts, including generalized pairs trading, cross-sectional analysis, principal component analysis (PCA), factor modeling, mean reversion dynamics, Ornstein-Uhlenbeck (OU) processes, trend-following approaches, the Kelly criterion, and trading parameter optimization‚Äîamong others.

These components are tightly integrated into a standalone, multi-stage framework that initially functions as a Trading Automaton‚Äîa meta-simulator designed to evaluate a wide range of trading strategies. Our workflow is structured around two core stages: a rigorous screening process and fully automated execution. We continuously integrate and test new methods by running extensive backtesting simulations on historical data. This produces a rich dataset that we use to refine our models and optimize overall system performance.

Our optimization approach combines established machine learning techniques with insights from cutting-edge AI research (eg. NLG, RAG, CAG, LLM pipelines, agent workflows), allowing us to refine strategies and adapt to evolving market conditions.

Among the micro-strategies currently incorporated into the software are approaches inspired by the work of Dr. Ernest Chan and the well-regarded paper "Statistical Arbitrage in the U.S. Equities Market" by Marco Avellaneda and Jeong-Hyun Lee. One strategy involves simulating trades across multiple historical time series data blocks and ranking profitable, low-risk Z-score signals using performance metrics such as the Sharpe ratio. Given the system's lower-frequency operation, full automation is not required, allowing for a degree of strategic discretion in trade execution.

The workflow continues to support discretionary order submission (i.e., clients conduct their own analysis and submit trades manually) while leveraging the strength of mean-reverting Z-score signals and their deviations from the mean. To refine the system's recommendations, additional layers of technical analysis‚Äîsuch as ARIMA, GARCH, and Markov-Switching Models (MSM)‚Äîare integrated. These signals and patterns can be further analyzed using large language models (LLMs) to generate deeper insight. We experiment with agentic LLM workflows and integrate Cache-Augmented Generation (CAG) and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) techniques, alongside custom LLM chatbot pipelines, to facilitate and enhance trade-related activities.

Example Manual Use Case

The time series data block was updated on the desired trade date, here we select an instance of Single-instrument Trader on S&P 500 data. (View output). Ignoring the table with quantitative metrics, we quickly scan the available Z-score signals. Notably, the DXCM Z-score sits just below the optimized entry condition.

Just above the output table are links to supporting analysis. Clicking the link for DXCM provides more insights: (DXCM stock analysis).

The Z-score itself doesn‚Äôt provide relative prices, but these are available in the first chart of the supporting analysis (shown below).

Suggested entry for a long position is $69.21, with an exit at $69.51. These values are optimized to minimize risk in automated trading. Deciding the entry is reasonable, before the market opens, we place a STOP LIMIT order through our trading platform. Assuming sufficient momentum, we add a take-profit order, setting it near the short exit target (around $70.10). After next day market close, as shown in the chart below, the trade was successful. However, we may have set the take profit to have been above the suggested short entry threshold. Interestingly, the price reversal began near the suggested short entry. NB: If we had stuck to the suggested long exit, we would have mitigated a lot of risk.
