In fact, by using this method, we not only develop a framework for how we might identify and dissect where and why portfolio drawdowns occur; Our scientists, engineers and developers can also set targets and objectives for performance enhancements with future software designs, fixes and upgrades.

What is Value at Risk (VaR)?

VaR represents the maximum expected loss at a specified confidence level over a given time period. For example, a 5% daily VaR of -10% means that, with 95% confidence, the portfolio will not lose more than 2% in the return period (eg. daily). However, there is a 5% chance that losses could exceed this threshold. To elaborate, if we rolled a 100 sided dice and got a number 1 through 5 then we might lose more than 10% of our invesment. We better hope we dont roll a 1 through 5 on the next day or the day after that (for more info on VaR, see this video)

Why Simulated Portfolios?

The data used in this graph comes from multiple simulated portfolio backtests. These backtests replicate the behavior a variety of micro-strategies under various conditions and asset combinations, offering a more comprehensive view of potential risks compared to a single time series. This ensures that the VaR estimate reflects the a good approximation for the variability and downside risks inherent in the intended automated excecution.

Interpreting the Graph

The histogram shows the distribution of daily percentage returns for all simulated portfolios.

  • The red dashed line indicates the VaR threshold at the specified confidence level (e.g., 95%). This line demarcates the point beyond which only a small percentage (e.g., 5%) of the returns fallâ€”representing the worst-case scenarios.
  • For instance, if the VaR at 95% confidence is -10.8%, it means 95% of the time, the daily loss will be less than 10.8%, but in the remaining 5%, it could be worse.

Limitations of VaR

VaR has the following limitations:

  • VaR does not predict the magnitude of losses beyond the threshold; it merely indicates that such losses are possible.
  • It assumes normal market conditions and might underestimate risk during extreme market events or black swan scenarios.
